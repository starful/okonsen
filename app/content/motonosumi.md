I’ve stood on many cliffsides in Japan, but nothing quite prepared me for the sight of Nagato. Imagine the wild, rhythmic crashing of the deep blue Sea of Japan, where white foam sprays against jagged rocks, and then—out of nowhere—a vivid, zigzagging line of 123 vermilion torii gates snaking down toward the water. It looks like a crimson dragon resting against the emerald hillside.

This is **Motonosumi Shrine (元乃隅神社)**. You might have seen it on CNN’s list of "Japan's 31 Most Beautiful Places," but I’m here to tell you that a photo, no matter how stunning, can’t capture the salt on your lips or the spiritual hum of this place. This isn't just a photo op; it’s a site born from a dream, guarded by a fox, and fueled by the quiet prayers of everyone who makes the trek to this remote corner of Yamaguchi.

---

### 1. A Miracle Built by Divine Revelation: The Deities and Origins

When I first visited, I wondered how such a dramatic shrine came to be. Usually, these sites are centuries old, but Motonosumi has a surprisingly modern, mystical origin story.

#### **A Message from the White Fox (Byakko)**
The heart of the shrine belongs to **Byakko Daishin**, the White Fox Deity. In Shinto tradition, foxes are the clever messengers of Inari (the god of prosperity). Here, the fox is your spiritual intermediary. Whether you’re praying for a new relationship, a safe pregnancy, or just a bit of good luck in your career, the white fox is the one who carries your whisper to the heavens.

#### **A History that Began in a Fisherman's Dream**
The story feels like a local folktale. Back in 1955, a local fisherman named Hitoshi Okamura had a vivid dream. A white fox appeared at his bedside, claiming it was the spirit from the famous Taikodani Inari Shrine in Shimane, and it was a bit miffed at being ignored!

The fox told him: *"I have protected your boats and your village for years. Enshrine me here."*

Taking the oracle to heart, Okamura and the villagers built this shrine right on the edge of the cliffs where the waves are fiercest. The name 'Motonosumi' literally means "Original Corner," marking its spot as the westernmost corner of Honshu. Since then, the village has thrived, and the 123 torii gates—donated by people whose wishes actually came true—now stand as a 100-meter "path of wishes."

💡 **Pro Tip:** Look closely at the pillars of the torii gates. You’ll see names and dates carved into them—each one represents a person who came here with a dream, just like you.

---

### 2. Walking the Path of the Red Dragon: A Stroll Through the Grounds and Power Spots

Walking through the grounds feels like moving through a living painting. The air is thick with the scent of the sea, and the wind here has a real "bite" to it that makes you feel alive.

#### **Where Sky and Sea Meet: The Honden (Main Hall)**
At the highest point, you’ll find the Main Hall. It’s modest, weathered by the salt and wind, which I think makes it feel even more sacred. When you stand here, try the traditional **Nirei-nihakushu-ichirei** (Two Bows, Two Claps, One Bow). 

There’s something incredibly grounding about clapping your hands while the ocean roars in the background. It feels like your prayer is being carried out to the horizon instantly.

#### **The 123 Red Tunnels: The Torii Path**
This is the "main event." Walking down through the red tunnel, with the cobalt sea on one side and lush green trees on the other, is surreal. The light flickers between the pillars, creating a strobe effect of shadows and bright vermilion. It’s a moment of pure "zen" where the noise of the world just fades away.

#### **Challenge! Japan's Hardest-to-Reach Saisen Bako**
You have to try this! Unlike most shrines where the offering box is at waist level, Motonosumi’s *saisen bako* is perched at the very top of a 6-meter-high torii gate. 

I watched a group of teenagers and grandmas alike tossing coins into the air, laughing and sighing as the wind blew their offerings off course. They say if you land your coin in the box, your wish is guaranteed. It took me four tries, but the cheer from the strangers standing around me when it finally "clinked" in was one of my favorite memories of the trip.

⚠️ **Heads up:** Don’t use your heavy 500-yen coins! A light 5-yen or 10-yen coin is much easier to toss (and less painful if you accidentally hit someone!).

#### **Nature's Breath: The Dragon Palace Geyser (Ryūgū no Shiofuki)**
Below the cliffs lies the *Ryūgū no Shiofuki*. When the waves hit the sea caves just right, water shoots up to 30 meters into the air like a geyser. If you visit in autumn or winter when the sea is rough, you’ll hear a thunderous *KWAAAAANG!*—it sounds exactly like a dragon roaring from the depths.

---

### 3. Proof of Worship: Goshuin and Souvenirs

I never leave a shrine without a **Goshuin** (temple stamp). The ones here are works of art—often featuring hand-drawn foxes and the winding torii gates. 

- **White Fox Amulets**: These are incredibly cute and serve as a "fulfillment of all wishes" charm.

- **The "Success" Amulet**: Specifically for those who managed to land their coin in that high-flying offering box!

💡 **Pro Tip:** The priest isn't always on-site. Look for the self-service station where you can get a beautiful pre-written Goshuin to paste into your book.

---

### 4. Deepening Your Journey: Nearby Recommended Spots

If you’ve driven all this way, don't just turn back! The surrounding coastline is breathtaking.

#### **1. Tsunoshima Bridge (角島大橋)**
About 25 minutes away, this bridge looks like something out of a luxury car commercial. The water is a surreal emerald green, and driving across it feels like you're gliding over the ocean.

#### **2. Senjojiki (千畳敷)**
A massive plateau 333 meters above sea level. It’s the perfect place to grab a coffee at 'Country Kitchen' and just stare at the horizon. It feels like you're standing at the edge of the world.

#### **3. The Taste of Nagato: Live Squid**
You haven't lived until you've tried *Kensaki-ika* (sword-tip squid) here. It’s served so fresh it’s still transparent. The sweetness is unlike any seafood I’ve had in Tokyo.

---

### 5. Access and Travel Tips

I'll be honest: getting here is a bit of an adventure. 

- **Rental Car:** This is your best bet. Set your GPS for **498 Yuyatsuo, Nagato-shi**. 

- **Taxi:** If you take the train to **Nagato-Furuichi Station**, you’ll need a 20-minute taxi ride.

- **⚠️ Heads up:** Taxis are rare here! Always grab the driver's business card or ask them to wait for you, otherwise, you might be stranded with the foxes!

### 6. ✨ Conclusion:

My trip to Motonosumi Shrine wasn't just about the photos; it was about the energy. Standing on that cliff, watching people toss their hopes into the sky and seeing the "red dragon" defy the crashing waves, reminded me of the beauty of human persistence. 

If you’re feeling a bit burnt out or just need to recharge your soul, make the trip to Yamaguchi. There’s a white fox waiting for you, a red path to walk, and a high-flying offering box just waiting for your "lucky" toss. I’m rooting for your wish to come true!
