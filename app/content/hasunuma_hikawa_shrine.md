### 1. 🙏 Deities and Origins: The Guardian of the Old Village

Hasunuma Hikawa Shrine (蓮沼氷川神社) is a classic example of a "Chinju" (tutelary shrine) that has watched over its specific local community for centuries. Like the many Hikawa shrines connected to the Musashi province (modern-day Tokyo and Saitama), it enshrines **Susanoo-no-Mikoto (素戔嗚尊)**.

Centuries ago, the Hasunuma area was a quiet farming village surrounded by wetlands and lotus ponds (which is the literal meaning of *Hasunuma*). The villagers established this shrine to pray to Susanoo-no-Mikoto for protection against floods, plagues, and misfortune (*Yakuyoke*). Today, the wetlands are gone, replaced by a bustling residential neighborhood, but the shrine remains the spiritual anchor for the locals, offering blessings for family safety and good health.

### 2. ⛩️ A Stroll Through the Precincts: A Personal Photo Diary

The beauty of Hasunuma Hikawa Shrine lies in its unassuming simplicity. There are no crowds of tourists here—only the quiet rustling of leaves, the sound of gravel underfoot, and the occasional resident stopping by to bow their head on their way home from work. The wooden main hall is well-preserved, and the stone torii gate stands as a steadfast boundary between the busy street and the sacred grounds. ### 3. 📜 Goshuin and Shrine Items: Simple Blessings

As a local neighborhood shrine, the shrine office (*Shamusho*) may not always be staffed during regular weekdays. However, during the New Year period or local festival days, you can receive a beautifully hand-stamped *Goshuin*. The *Omamori* (amulets) offered here focus primarily on *Kanai Anzen* (Safety of the Family) and *Yaku-yoke* (Warding off evil), perfectly reflecting its role as a local protector.

***

### 4. 🌃 What to See Nearby: Shimizuzaka Park

If you are looking for a place to relax after visiting the shrine, take a short walk toward the border of Kita-ku to visit **Shimizuzaka Park (清水坂公園)**. This unique park makes excellent use of the area's natural hilly terrain. It features lush greenery, a rushing artificial mountain stream, and a famous, massive 52-meter-long roller slide that is incredibly fun (even for adults!). It is an excellent spot to enjoy some nature and fresh air.

### ✨ Editor's Closing Remarks

Hasunuma Hikawa Shrine is the kind of place you stumble upon when you simply decide to take a walk through a Tokyo residential neighborhood without a map. It doesn't command your attention with flashy colors, but it invites you in with a profound sense of calm. I found myself sitting on a bench near the entrance for quite a while, just enjoying the slow pace of the afternoon. If you are exploring the Toei Mita Line area and need a brief, meditative break from the concrete jungle, this shrine offers the perfect little escape.

***

### ♨️ Relax at a Nearby Onsen: Sakae-yu (栄湯)

Just a few streets away from the shrine in Hasunumacho, you will find **Sakae-yu**, a fantastic neighborhood *sento* (public bath). Don't let the word "local" fool you—Sakae-yu was beautifully renovated in recent years and features a very clean, modern interior with soft lighting. They offer highly relaxing micro-bubble baths and a sauna. Taking a hot bath here, surrounded by the friendly chatter of the local community, is the ultimate way to cap off your authentic Itabashi experience.
